   crdc_join_for_filters <-
    data1
   schl_loc_combo <- data2 |>
     mutate(COMBOKEY = NCESSCH)
   
   crdc_join_county <- schl_loc_combo |>
     inner_join(crdc_join_for_filters, by = "COMBOKEY")

}

For this analysis, we chose to focus on mechanical restraint in Harris County, Texas (county in Texas with the highest total Black or African American population). For the plot, we examined how the percent of students that were mechanically restrained differed across race and gender. For the map, we examined the percent of Black or African American students throughout Harris County. In addition to this, we also showcased the percent of Black or African American students that were mechanically restrained at each school in the county.

When looking at the plot, in Harris County, Texas the race and gender with the highest percent of students mechanically restrained was Hispanic or Latino male. The second highest race and gender was Black or African American Females. When comparing these percentages to White students, it appears that they are almost double. This suggests that there may be racial disparities occurring for (Hispanic or Latino and Black and African American) students with the use of mechanical restraint in the county. When looking at the map, there are many schools that appear white (0.0 - 0.2) or grey (NA) when looking at mechanical restraint percentages. This showcases that there is not a lot of data related to mechanical restraint in this county. However, in one instance, there is a school (circle) that is of a darker blue shade (0.8 - 1.0). This may suggest that there are instances of racial discrimination occurring at this particular school in Harris County.

When thinking about our plot, it is clear that there are higher percentages of mechanical restraint occurring for Hispanic or Latino males and Black or African American females. In addition to this, through looking at our map, though there is not a lot of data related to mechanical restraint in Harris county, there are some schools in tracts (North and South of Houston) that have high population percentages for Black and African Americans. When looking at the CRDC datasets, it is important to note that specific schools are excluded. The dataset does not include schools that are not receiving federal assistance from the CRDC. On the United States Department of Education website, it says tribal schools are not required to collect and submit data to the CRDC. In addition to this, schools in United States territories are also excluded from the dataset as they are not required to submit data to the CRDC. When looking at the Restraint and Seclusion dataset, gender is only split into two categories M (male) and F (female). The dataset fails to include all gender identities, such as transgender and nonbinary. A benefit of these datasets is they can be used to highlight disparities in schools. With clear data on specific issues, more funding and resources can be given to schools that need assistance. Though the dataset was created to aid in prohibiting discrimination, it is harmful because it does not include representation for all people. Specific groups of students, like transgender and nonbinary students, may not receive help or be recognized because they are excluded from the dataset.
